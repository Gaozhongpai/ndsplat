\begin{abstract}
High-dimensional Gaussian Splatting methods like 6DGS and 7DGS achieve impressive view-dependent and dynamic scene modeling through conditional N-dimensional Gaussians. However, their monolithic covariance design enforces tight coupling between position shifts and opacity modulation, limiting flexibility and requiring computational overhead that accumulates across millions of primitives during training. We introduce \modelname{}, a disentangled parameterization that separates these conditioning effects into independent modules with learnable coupling. Our key contribution is per-Gaussian parameters that adaptively interpolate between fully independent position learning and coupled conditional behavior, eliminating the need for matrix inversions while enabling flexible control. Position shifts use direct displacement matrices, and opacity modulation employs Cholesky precision factors for numerically stable distance computation. This disentanglement achieves up to 17\% higher rendering throughput across diverse benchmarks while matching or exceeding N-DGS quality. The learned coupling parameters provide interpretable scene decomposition, revealing which regions benefit from entangled versus independent conditioning. Our approach offers a principled, efficient alternative to monolithic covariance representations in high-dimensional Gaussian splatting.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS)~\citep{3dgs} revolutionized novel view synthesis by achieving photorealistic quality at real-time speeds through explicit 3D Gaussian primitives. However, real-world scenes exhibit complex phenomena beyond static representations: specular reflections, temporal dynamics, and view-dependent effects that require richer modeling capacity.

Recent extensions elevate Gaussians into higher-dimensional spaces to capture these phenomena. N-dimensional Gaussians (N-DG)~\citep{diolatzis2024n} pioneered this direction, refined by 6D Gaussian Splatting (6DGS)~\citep{gao20246dgs} which models position and viewing direction jointly for view-dependent effects, and 7D Gaussian Splatting (7DGS)~\citep{gao20257dgs} which adds temporal dimensions for dynamic scenes. Alternative kernel designs like Deformable Beta Splatting (DBS)~\citep{liu2025deformable} improve geometric fidelity but remain limited to spatial dimensions.

Despite their expressive power, these N-dimensional formulations suffer from two fundamental limitations. First, they require inverting covariance sub-matrices ($O(C^3)$ operations per Gaussian) in every forward pass, creating computational overhead that accumulates across millions of primitives during training. Second, their monolithic covariance structure enforces tight coupling: position shifts and opacity modulation are entangled through a shared precision matrix. This coupling limits flexibility: some scene regions may benefit from independent opacity control without position displacement (e.g., view-dependent shading on static geometry), while others require coupled behavior (e.g., specular highlights that move and fade together). The rigid entanglement also risks numerical instabilities when matrices become ill-conditioned.

\textbf{Our Insight.} The full N-dimensional joint covariance matrix (where $N = 3 + C$, with 3 spatial dimensions plus $C$ conditioning dimensions for view/time) is unnecessarily complex. Position shifts and opacity modulation serve distinct purposes and can be \textbf{disentangled} into independent modules, each with efficient direct parameterization. Rather than deriving all effects from a shared covariance and computing matrix inversions, we parameterize each component separately with learnable coupling between them. This design mirrors \textit{factorized attention} mechanisms: view/time act as \textit{queries} that independently modulate \textit{values} (position and opacity), creating sparse pathways instead of the dense coupling in N-DGS. This factorization enables extensibility to additional queries (lighting, materials) and values (rotation, color) without combinatorial complexity.

We introduce \textbf{\modelname{}}, a disentangled parameterization for efficient conditional Gaussian splatting. The `\textbf{d}' signifies our principle: \textbf{d}isentangle conditioning into independent components with \textbf{d}irect parameterization---\textbf{d}isplacement matrices for position and \textbf{d}ecay factors for opacity. Our key contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Direct Opacity Parameterization:} We directly parameterize opacity decay via Cholesky-factored precision matrices, replacing $O(C^3)$ matrix inversions with $O(C^2)$ triangular operations while guaranteeing numerical stability.

\item \textbf{Scaled Position Displacement:} Position shifts are parameterized by diagonal scaling with bounded learnable parameters, ensuring tight, symmetric displacement bounds and avoiding asymmetries from N-DGS's implicit coupling structure.

\item \textbf{Learnable Coupling:} Per-Gaussian coupling parameters enable adaptive interpolation between fully independent and coupled behavior, allowing each primitive to learn its optimal disentanglement strength.

\item \textbf{Efficient CUDA Implementation:} We implement optimized CUDA kernels for both our method and N-DGS baseline, fusing conditional operations with register-level computation for fair comparison (Note that the original N-DGS only implements in PyTorch).
\end{enumerate}

Experiments demonstrate that \modelname{} matches or exceeds N-DGS quality across all benchmarks while achieving up to 17\% higher rendering throughput.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

\textbf{3D Gaussian Splatting and Extensions.}
3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS)~\cite{3dgs} revolutionized novel view synthesis by representing scenes as collections of anisotropic 3D Gaussians, enabling real-time rendering with competitive quality. The method's success has inspired numerous extensions addressing anti-aliasing~\cite{yu2023mipsplattingaliasfree3dgaussian,yan2024multiscale3dgaussiansplatting}, compression~\cite{lee2024compact,morgenstern2024compact}, and semantic applications~\cite{shi2023languageembedded3dgaussians,zhou2024feature3dgssupercharging3d}. However, 3DGS's fixed Gaussian primitives and spherical harmonic color encoding struggle with complex view-dependent effects and dynamic scenes.

\textbf{N-Dimensional Gaussian Methods.}
To address view-dependent effects, N-dimensional Gaussians (N-DG)~\cite{diolatzis2024n} extended Gaussian representations into higher dimensions, refined by 6D Gaussian Splatting (6DGS)~\cite{gao20246dgs} which models position and viewing direction jointly. 7D Gaussian Splatting (7DGS)~\cite{gao20257dgs} further incorporates temporal dimensions for dynamic scenes. These methods achieve impressive results but their monolithic covariance design creates computational overhead and can introduce numerical instabilities when covariance matrices become ill-conditioned.

\textbf{Efficient Parameterizations.}
Various parameterization strategies have been explored for covariance matrices in Gaussian splatting. The original 3DGS uses quaternion-based rotation with diagonal scaling, ensuring positive semi-definiteness while maintaining interpretability. For higher dimensions, methods resort to Cholesky factorization, which loses explicit orthogonality but generalizes to arbitrary dimensions. Recent work on deformable splatting~\cite{liu2025deformable} explores adaptive kernel shapes, though these remain limited to spatial dimensions without addressing the coupling problem in conditional distributions.

\textbf{Dynamic Scene Modeling.}
Early NeRF extensions modeled dynamics through deformation fields: D-NeRF~\cite{pumarola2020dnerfneuralradiancefields}, Nerfies~\cite{park2021nerfies}, and HyperNeRF~\cite{park2021hypernerf}. For Gaussian Splatting, 4D Gaussian Splatting (4DGS)~\cite{yang2023real} extends primitives temporally, while Dynamic 3D Gaussians~\cite{luiten2023dynamic} combines canonical representations with learned deformations. 7DGS unifies spatial, temporal, and angular dimensions but enforces tight coupling through shared covariance structures, preventing independent control of position and opacity modulation.

Our work addresses these limitations by introducing learnable disentanglement between position and opacity conditioning, eliminating matrix inversions while enabling adaptive coupling control through per-Gaussian parameters.

\section{Preliminary}

Our work builds upon the family of Gaussian Splatting methods. To contextualize our contribution, we first detail the progression from static 3D models to the more complex 6D and 7D formulations, highlighting the trade-offs between expressive power and computational cost at each step.

\paragraph{3D Gaussian Splatting}
3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) revolutionized novel view synthesis by representing scenes as a collection of anisotropic 3D Gaussians. Each Gaussian is a primitive defined by a set of learnable attributes that describe its geometry and appearance. Its geometry is determined by a mean vector \(\boldsymbol{\mu} \in \mathbb{R}^3\) specifying its center in 3D space, and a covariance matrix \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}\) defining its shape, scale, and orientation. To ensure physical plausibility and simplify optimization, \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) is parameterized by a scaling matrix \(\mathbf{S} = \operatorname{diag}(s_x, s_y, s_z)\) and a rotation matrix \(\mathbf{R}\) (represented as a quaternion), such that \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \mathbf{R} \mathbf{S} \mathbf{S}^\top \mathbf{R}^\top\). The appearance of each Gaussian is governed by a scalar opacity \(\alpha \in [0, 1]\) that controls its transparency, and a view-dependent color modeled using Spherical Harmonics (SH). The color \(c\) for a given viewing direction \(d\) is computed as a sum over the SH basis functions \(Y_{\ell m}\):
\begin{equation}
c(d) = \sum_{\ell=0}^{N} \sum_{m=-\ell}^{\ell} \beta_{\ell m} Y_{\ell m}(d),
\end{equation}
where \(\beta_{\ell m}\) are the learnable SH coefficients. 3DGS achieves state-of-the-art quality and real-time rendering for static scenes. However, its representation is fundamentally static and cannot model geometric changes arising from complex view-dependent effects or temporal dynamics.

\paragraph{N-Dimensional Gaussian Splatting}

To capture view-dependent and temporal effects, recent methods extend 3DGS into higher dimensions: 6DGS for view-dependent phenomena and 7DGS for dynamic scenes. Each primitive is an N-dimensional Gaussian over position $\mathbf{X}_p \in \mathbb{R}^3$ and query variables $\mathbf{X}_q \in \mathbb{R}^C$ (view direction for 6DGS with $C=3$, view+time for 7DGS with $C=4$):
\begin{align}
\mathbf{X} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{X}_p \\ \mathbf{X}_q \end{pmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(
\begin{pmatrix}
\boldsymbol{\mu}_p \\ \boldsymbol{\mu}_q
\end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix}
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pp} & \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pq} \\
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pq}^\top & \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{qq}
\end{pmatrix}
\right).
\end{align}
At query $\mathbf{q}$, conditioning yields a 3D Gaussian with parameters:
\begin{align}
\alpha_{\text{cond}} &= \alpha \cdot \exp\left(-\lambda\,(\mathbf{q}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_q)^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{qq}^{-1}(\mathbf{q}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_q)\right),\\
\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{cond}} &= \boldsymbol{\mu}_p + \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pq}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{qq}^{-1}(\mathbf{q}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_q), \\
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\text{cond}} &= \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pp} - \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pq}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{qq}^{-1}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pq}^\top,
\end{align}
where $\lambda$ is a scaling factor (default 0.35). This formulation requires inverting $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{qq}$ ($O(C^3)$ operations) for every Gaussian in every forward pass, causing computational overhead and potential numerical instability during training. This motivates our simpler parameterization.

\section{Our Approach}

The full covariance formulations of 6DGS and 7DGS model all conditioning effects through a single $(3+C) \times (3+C)$ joint covariance matrix. To compute any single effect (position shift, opacity modulation, or covariance adjustment), one must invert the entire $C \times C$ conditioning block $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{qq}$.

We propose \modelname{}, which \textbf{disentangles} these effects into three independent modules. Rather than deriving all transformations from a shared covariance structure, we directly parameterize each conditioning component separately. This disentanglement eliminates the need for full covariance modeling while enabling efficient, modular computation.

\subsection{Overview}

Our approach reformulates conditional Gaussian slicing into two \textbf{disentangled} components. Given the deviation $\boldsymbol{\delta} = q - \mu_q$ from the canonical view/time:
\begin{align}
\alpha_{\text{cond}} &= \alpha \cdot \exp\left(-\lambda \cdot \|\mathbf{L}^\top \boldsymbol{\delta}\|_2^2\right), & &\text{(Opacity)} \\
\mu_{\text{cond}} &= \mu_p + \mathbf{V}_{pq} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{V}}_{qq}^{-1} \cdot \boldsymbol{\delta}, & &\text{(Position)} \end{align}
where $\mathbf{L} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times C}$ is the Cholesky factor of the precision matrix $\mathbf{V}_{qq}^{-1} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^\top$, $\mathbf{V}_{pq} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times C}$ is the displacement matrix, $\lambda_\text{pos} \in [0,1]$ is a learnable per-Gaussian parameter controlling position-opacity coupling, and $\lambda$ is a global opacity scaling hyperparameter (default 0.35) that controls the rate of opacity decay with distance. The scaled precision matrix $\tilde{\mathbf{V}}_{qq}^{-1}$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathbf{V}}_{qq}^{-1} = (1-\lambda_\text{pos}) \mathbf{I} + \lambda_\text{pos} \mathbf{V}_{qq}^{-1}.
\end{equation}
The spatial covariance $\Sigma_{pp}$ remains independent of the query, using the standard 3DGS quaternion-scale parameterization ($\Sigma_{pp} = RSS^\top R^\top$) for seamless compatibility with existing pipelines, including densification strategies.

For 7DGS ($C=4$), we further \textbf{separate view and time} by using a block-diagonal precision matrix with zero cross-terms:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{V}_{qq}^{-1} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^\top = \begin{pmatrix}
\mathbf{\Sigma}_{vv}^{-1} & \mathbf{0} \\
\mathbf{0} & \boldsymbol{\sigma}_t^{-1}
\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{\Sigma}_{vv}^{-1} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$ is the view precision and $\boldsymbol{\sigma}_t^{-1} \in \mathbb{R}$ is the time precision. This separation eliminates coupling between spatial view directions and temporal dynamics, reflecting the physical intuition that view-dependent effects and temporal changes are independent phenomena.

The key innovation is \textbf{learnable position-opacity coupling} via the scaled precision matrix $\tilde{\mathbf{V}}_{qq}^{-1}$: the parameter $\lambda_{\text{pos}}$ enables each Gaussian to adaptively interpolate between fully independent ($\lambda_{\text{pos}}=0$) and coupled N-DGS behavior ($\lambda_{\text{pos}}=1$). The following subsections detail how this learnable coupling is implemented for opacity and position conditioning.

\subsection{Opacity Conditioning}

In N-DGS, opacity is modulated as $\alpha_{\text{cond}} = \alpha \cdot \exp(-\lambda\,(\mathbf{q}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_q)^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{qq}^{-1}(\mathbf{q}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_q))$, requiring matrix inversion.

Our formulation computes $\mathbf{L}^\top \boldsymbol{\delta}$ followed by $\|\cdot\|_2^2$ through a triangular matrix-vector multiplication that is highly parallelizable on GPUs. This simpler operation avoids the pivoting and numerical stability checks required for matrix inversion. Parameterizing the Cholesky factor directly with exponential activation on the diagonal guarantees that $\mathbf{V}_{qq}^{-1} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^\top$ is always symmetric positive definite, eliminating numerical instabilities from ill-conditioned covariance matrices.

For 7DGS, the block-diagonal structure enables \textbf{separated view and time opacity}. With $\boldsymbol{\delta} = (\boldsymbol{\delta}_v, \delta_t)^\top$ where $\boldsymbol{\delta}_v \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is the view deviation and $\delta_t \in \mathbb{R}$ is the time deviation, the opacity factors as:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_{\text{cond}} = \alpha \cdot \exp\left(-\lambda\left(\boldsymbol{\delta}_v^\top \mathbf{\Sigma}_{vv}^{-1} \boldsymbol{\delta}_v + \delta_t^2 \boldsymbol{\sigma}_t^{-1}\right)\right).
\end{equation}
This factorization allows view-dependent and time-dependent opacity to be controlled independently, with view precision $\mathbf{\Sigma}_{vv}^{-1}$ parameterized by a $3 \times 3$ Cholesky factor (6 parameters) and time precision $\boldsymbol{\sigma}_t^{-1}$ by a single scalar, for a total of 7 parameters instead of 10 for a full $4 \times 4$ precision matrix.

\subsection{Position Conditioning}
\label{subsec:position}

In N-DGS, position is computed as $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{cond}} = \boldsymbol{\mu}_p + \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pq}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{qq}^{-1}(\mathbf{q}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_q)$, where $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pq}$ is derived from a joint Cholesky decomposition and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{qq}^{-1}$ is the precision matrix that also controls opacity. This coupling means position shifts and opacity modulation are entangled through the shared precision matrix.

We introduce \textbf{learnable position-opacity disentanglement} through a scaled precision matrix:
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{cond}} = \boldsymbol{\mu}_p + \mathbf{V}_{pq} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{V}}_{qq}^{-1} \cdot \boldsymbol{\delta}, \quad \text{where} \quad \tilde{\mathbf{V}}_{qq}^{-1} = (1-\lambda_\text{pos}) \mathbf{I} + \lambda_\text{pos} \mathbf{V}_{qq}^{-1},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{V}_{pq} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times C}$ is the displacement matrix, $\lambda_\text{pos} \in [0,1]$ is a learnable per-Gaussian parameter, and $\mathbf{V}_{qq}^{-1} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^\top$ is the precision matrix (derived from Cholesky factor $\mathbf{L}$) that also governs opacity. The scaled precision matrix $\tilde{\mathbf{V}}_{qq}^{-1}$ controls the coupling strength:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item When $\lambda_\text{pos}=0$: $\tilde{\mathbf{V}}_{qq}^{-1} = \mathbf{I}$, so position shift reduces to $\mathbf{V}_{pq} \cdot \boldsymbol{\delta}$, fully disentangled from the precision matrix $\mathbf{V}_{qq}^{-1} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^\top$ that controls opacity.
\item When $\lambda_\text{pos}=1$: $\tilde{\mathbf{V}}_{qq}^{-1} = \mathbf{V}_{qq}^{-1}$, recovering the N-DGS formulation $\mathbf{V}_{pq} \cdot \mathbf{V}_{qq}^{-1} \cdot \boldsymbol{\delta}$, where position and opacity are coupled through the shared precision matrix.
\item Intermediate values: Partial coupling, blending independent position learning with conditional Gaussian behavior.
\end{itemize}
This provides adaptive disentanglement where each Gaussian learns its optimal coupling strength during training.

Following N-DGS's design principle that displacement should scale with spatial extent (so small Gaussians produce small displacements), we parameterize the displacement matrix with bounded activation and explicit per-dimension spatial scaling. We directly learn $\mathbf{V}_{pq} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times C}$ as:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{V}_{pq} = \mathbf{S} \boldsymbol{\Theta}_{pq},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{S} = \text{diag}(s_x, s_y, s_z) \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$ is the diagonal scale matrix from the Gaussian parameterization and $\boldsymbol{\Theta}_{pq} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times C}$ is a learnable parameter matrix with $\tanh(\cdot)$ activation applied element-wise. The tanh activation naturally bounds each element to $[-1, 1]$, ensuring that displacements in each spatial dimension scale proportionally with the corresponding Gaussian extent. This provides an inductive bias coupling displacement scale to Gaussian anisotropy, while the learnable parameters $\boldsymbol{\Theta}_{pq}$ allow each Gaussian to adaptively learn displacement direction and magnitude jointly. (See Appendix~\ref{sec:spatial_scaling_proof} for detailed comparison showing our explicit parameterization provides tighter displacement bounds than N-DGS.)


\subsection{CUDA Implementation}

Since the original N-DGS methods (6DGS and 7DGS) use PyTorch's autograd for conditional Gaussian operations without custom CUDA kernels, we implement both our disentangled formulation and the N-DGS baseline with optimized CUDA kernels for fair comparison. Our implementation uses a custom kernel that fuses conditional position and opacity computations in a single pass, as shown in Algorithm~\ref{alg:slice}.

\textbf{CUDA Kernel Design.} The kernel is templated on dimension $C$ (3 for 6DGS, 4 for 7DGS) with compile-time loop unrolling for optimal performance. Each thread processes one Gaussian primitive independently, enabling perfect parallelization across millions of Gaussians. For opacity computation (Line 2 in Algorithm~\ref{alg:slice}), we compute the triangular matrix-vector product $\mathbf{L}^\top \boldsymbol{\delta}$ directly in registers using the lower-triangular structure—only $C(C+1)/2$ multiplications are needed (6 for 6DGS, 10 for 7DGS), followed by a dot product for $\|\cdot\|_2^2$. For position displacement (Lines 3-5), we construct $\mathbf{V}_{pq}$ via diagonal scaling of $\tanh$ activations, then compute the scaled precision matrix $\tilde{\mathbf{V}}_{qq}^{-1}$ by interpolating between identity and $\mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^\top$ using the coupling parameter $\lambda_{\text{pos}}$. All matrix operations use registers rather than shared memory, as the small matrix sizes fit entirely in per-thread registers. This fused design minimizes global memory bandwidth by avoiding intermediate tensor allocations and maximizes GPU occupancy through register-level computation.

\textbf{Modular Compatibility.} Our implementation provides compile-time options to enable different levels of conditioning: (1) standard 3DGS with no conditioning, (2) \textit{d}GS-O with opacity modulation only, and (3) full \textit{d}GS with both opacity and position modulation. This modularity maintains compatibility with existing 3DGS codebases while allowing practitioners to select the appropriate complexity-performance tradeoff for their application.


\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Conditional Gaussian Slicing}
\label{alg:slice}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Per-Gaussian parameters: $\mu_p$, $\mu_q$, $\alpha$, scale $\mathbf{S}$, $\boldsymbol{\Theta}_{pq}$, Cholesky $\mathbf{L}$, $\lambda_{\text{pos}}$
\Require Query point: $\text{query}$ (view direction for 6DGS, or view+time for 7DGS)
\State $\boldsymbol{\delta} \gets \text{query} - \mu_q$ \Comment{Compute query deviation}
\State $\alpha_{\text{cond}} \gets \alpha \cdot \exp(-\lambda \cdot \|\mathbf{L}^\top \boldsymbol{\delta}\|^2)$ \Comment{Conditional opacity ($\lambda=0.35$)}
\State $\mathbf{V}_{pq} \gets \mathbf{S} \cdot \boldsymbol{\Theta}_{pq}$ \Comment{Construct displacement matrix (with tanh activation)}
\State $\tilde{\mathbf{V}}_{qq}^{-1} \gets (1-\lambda_{\text{pos}}) \mathbf{I} + \lambda_{\text{pos}} \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^\top$ \Comment{Scaled precision for coupling}
\State $\mu_{\text{cond}} \gets \mu_p + \mathbf{V}_{pq} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{V}}_{qq}^{-1} \cdot \boldsymbol{\delta}$ \Comment{Conditional position}
\State \Return $\alpha_{\text{cond}}$, $\mu_{\text{cond}}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Datasets.}
We evaluate \modelname{} across five diverse datasets covering both static and dynamic scenes:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textit{Static scenes:} (1) \textit{NeRF Synthetic}~\cite{mildenhall2021nerf} with 8 synthetic scenes under controlled lighting for geometric reconstruction assessment; (2) \textit{Mip-NeRF 360}~\cite{barron2021mipnerf} with 9 unbounded real-world scenes challenging view synthesis; and (3) \textit{6DGS PBR}~\cite{gao20246dgs} featuring 7 physically-based scenes with complex view-dependent effects.

    \item \textit{Dynamic scenes:} (4) \textit{D-NeRF}~\cite{pumarola2020dnerfneuralradiancefields} with 8 synthetic sequences featuring various deformation types; and (5) \textit{7DGS-PBR}~\cite{gao20257dgs} containing 6 physically-based dynamic scenes with complex spatio-temporal-angular correlations.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Evaluation Metrics.}
We assess reconstruction quality using Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR), Structural Similarity Index (SSIM)~\cite{wang2004image}, and Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS)~\cite{zhang2018perceptual}. We also report rendering speed (FPS), training time (minutes), and Gaussian count (K) to evaluate computational efficiency.

\textbf{Implementation Details.}
We compare three configurations: (1) \textbf{N-DGS}: full N-DGS formulation with joint position and opacity conditioning (entangled baseline), (2) \textbf{\textit{d}GS-O}: our disentangled approach with opacity conditioning only ($\mu_{\text{cond}} = \mu_p$, no position displacement), and (3) \textbf{\textit{d}GS}: our full disentangled approach with both opacity conditioning and learnable position coupling via $\lambda_\text{pos}$. Training uses Adam optimizer for 30K iterations. Proper initialization is critical for stable training: we initialize the view mean $\mu_d$ as random unit vectors (with time in $[0, 1]$ for 7DGS), the displacement parameters $\boldsymbol{\Theta}_{pq}$ from $\mathcal{N}(0, 0.01^2)$ to start with small displacements, and the Cholesky factor $\mathbf{L}$ with diagonal elements $\log(2.0)$ and off-diagonal elements 0.

\subsection{Results and Analysis}

The comparison between our three configurations reveals distinct efficiency-quality trade-offs. \textbf{\textit{d}GS-O} uses only opacity conditioning with $\mu_{\text{cond}} = \mu_p$ (no position displacement). On static scenes (NeRF Synthetic, Mip-NeRF 360, 6DGS PBR), it achieves strong quality: +0.46 dB on NeRF Synthetic, +0.78 dB on Mip-NeRF 360, but -0.57 dB on 6DGS PBR compared to N-DGS. The efficiency gains are substantial: 23\% faster rendering (754.98 vs 615.57 FPS on NeRF Synthetic), 89\% faster on Mip-NeRF 360 (279.30 vs 147.46 FPS). Notably, when primitive count is large (e.g., \texttt{Mip-NeRF 360}), \textit{d}GS-O achieves 89\% faster rendering despite using 86\% more primitives (2363K vs 1273K), demonstrating that computational efficiency is dominated by per-primitive operations rather than primitive count. However, on dynamic scenes, the lack of position displacement significantly impacts quality: -2.21 dB on D-NeRF, indicating that temporal dynamics benefit more from position conditioning than static view-dependent effects.

\textbf{\textit{d}GS} adds learnable position coupling via $\lambda_{\text{pos}}$, enabling each Gaussian to adaptively balance between fully independent position shifts ($\lambda_{\text{pos}}=0$, where position is decoupled from opacity-controlling precision) and coupled N-DGS behavior ($\lambda_{\text{pos}}=1$, where both share the same precision matrix). This recovers quality losses: on D-NeRF it achieves +0.39 dB over N-DGS (33.52 vs 33.13 dB), demonstrating that learnable coupling can even surpass the entangled baseline. On static scenes, \textit{d}GS consistently matches or exceeds N-DGS (+0.46 dB on NeRF Synthetic, +0.93 dB on Mip-NeRF 360, +0.66 dB on 6DGS PBR), while maintaining competitive rendering speeds (687.87 FPS on NeRF Synthetic, 12\% faster than N-DGS).

For 7DGS PBR, the results are highly competitive: \textit{d}GS-O achieves 31.70 dB (only -0.05 dB vs N-DGS at 31.75 dB) while \textit{d}GS matches this at 31.72 dB (-0.03 dB). Both variants deliver substantial efficiency gains, with \textit{d}GS-O achieving 18\% faster rendering (835.24 vs 709.83 FPS) and 9\% faster training (34.58 vs 38.14 min). The minimal quality gap demonstrates that learnable coupling effectively adapts to scenes with complex spatio-temporal-angular correlations while maintaining the efficiency benefits of disentangled parameterization.

\subsection{Ablation Study}

To understand the contribution of each component in our approach, we conduct an ablation study comparing three configurations: (1) \textbf{\textit{d}GS-O}: opacity conditioning only without position displacement, (2) \textbf{\textit{d}GS w/o $\mathbf{S}$}: position displacement without spatial scaling, and (3) \textbf{\textit{d}GS}: our full method with per-dimension spatial scaling. Notably, position displacement without proper scaling (dGS w/o $\mathbf{S}$) performs worse than using no position displacement at all (dGS-O), with drops of -2.58 dB on NeRF Synthetic and -1.76 dB on 6DGS PBR. This validates our design choice to respect Gaussian anisotropy in the displacement parameterization.

\section{Future Work}

The disentangled framework naturally extends in two directions, analogous to factorized attention mechanisms where opacity and position are \textbf{values} independently modulated by view and time as \textbf{query/key}. We can extend both the \textit{value space} (what gets modulated) and the \textit{query space} (what drives modulation). For values, we can add \textbf{rotation conditioning} (for orientation-dependent effects) or \textbf{color modulation} (for appearance variations). For queries, we can incorporate \textbf{lighting direction} (for relighting) or \textbf{material properties} (for physically-based effects). Each new dimension---whether value or query---can be incorporated as an independent module with its own learnable coupling parameter, avoiding the combinatorial complexity of full joint covariance matrices. For instance, rotation conditioning parameterizes orientation changes via quaternion interpolation $\mathbf{q}_{\text{cond}} = \mathbf{q} \otimes \text{slerp}(\mathbf{I}, \mathbf{q}_\delta, 1 - \alpha_{\text{rot}})$, where $\mathbf{q}$ is the base rotation, $\mathbf{q}_\delta$ is a learnable rotation offset, $\alpha_{\text{rot}}$ is a query-dependent weight computed from the query (analogous to but separate from the position/opacity weights), and $\mathbf{I}$ is the identity quaternion. The interpolation weight $(1 - \alpha_{\text{rot}})$ ties rotation strength to the query, enabling view/time-dependent orientation as an independent module without requiring joint covariance with position or opacity dimensions.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented \modelname{}, a disentangled parameterization for efficient N-dimensional Gaussian splatting. Our key insight is that the full joint covariance matrix is unnecessarily complex---position shifts and opacity modulation can be disentangled into independent modules with efficient direct parameterization and learnable coupling. This approach delivers efficiency by replacing cubic-complexity inversions with linear operations, stability through direct Cholesky parameterization, and interpretability via scene-adaptive coupling that reveals which regions benefit from entangled versus independent conditioning. Experiments demonstrate up to 17\% higher rendering throughput while matching or exceeding N-DGS quality across diverse benchmarks. The disentangled framework naturally extends to additional conditioning dimensions like rotation, lighting, and materials, providing a flexible foundation for future high-dimensional Gaussian representations.

\section{Comparison to N-DGS Displacement Parameterization}
\label{sec:spatial_scaling_proof}

Both N-DGS and \modelname{} scale displacement with spatial scale $s_i$, but differ in how tightly this coupling is achieved and in their parameterization approach.

\textbf{N-DGS: Implicit coupling via Cholesky structure.} N-DGS parameterizes the full $(3+C) \times (3+C)$ covariance via Cholesky decomposition $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^\top$, where $\mathbf{L}$ is lower triangular with diagonal elements $L_{ii} = s_i$ (spatial and query scales) and off-diagonal elements $L_{ij} = \ell_{ij}$ for $i > j$, where $\ell_{ij} \in [-1,1]$ are bounded correlation parameters (initialized to zero).

To illustrate this structure, consider 6DGS with 3 spatial and 3 view dimensions ($C=3$). The Cholesky factor $\mathbf{L} \in \mathbb{R}^{6 \times 6}$ has the form:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{L} = \left(\begin{array}{rrrrrr}
       s_1 &        0 &        0 &        0 &        0 &        0 \\
\ell_{21} &       s_2 &        0 &        0 &        0 &        0 \\
\ell_{31} & \ell_{32} &       s_3 &        0 &        0 &        0 \\
\ell_{41} & \ell_{42} & \ell_{43} &       s_4 &        0 &        0 \\
\ell_{51} & \ell_{52} & \ell_{53} & \ell_{54} &       s_5 &        0 \\
\ell_{61} & \ell_{62} & \ell_{63} & \ell_{64} & \ell_{65} &       s_6
\end{array}\right),
\end{equation}
where $s_1, s_2, s_3$ are spatial scales, $s_4, s_5, s_6$ are query dimension scales, and $\ell_{ij} \in [-1,1]$ are bounded correlations.

The displacement block $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pq} = (\mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^\top)_{1:3,4:6}$ emerges from matrix multiplication. Computing via row-column dot products yields:
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pq} = \left(\begin{array}{rrr}
                           s_1 \ell_{41} &                            s_1 \ell_{51} &                            s_1 \ell_{61} \\
     \ell_{21}\ell_{41} + s_2 \ell_{42} &      \ell_{21}\ell_{51} + s_2 \ell_{52} &      \ell_{21}\ell_{61} + s_2 \ell_{62} \\
\ell_{31}\ell_{41} + \ell_{32}\ell_{42} + s_3 \ell_{43} & \ell_{31}\ell_{51} + \ell_{32}\ell_{52} + s_3 \ell_{53} & \ell_{31}\ell_{61} + \ell_{32}\ell_{62} + s_3 \ell_{63}
\end{array}\right).
\end{equation}
Note that $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pq}$ contains only spatial scales $s_1, s_2, s_3$ and bounded correlations $\ell_{ij}$ -- the query dimension scales $s_4, s_5, s_6$ do \textit{not} appear. This occurs because displacement describes how spatial position changes with query deviation, which should scale with the Gaussian's \textit{spatial} extent, not its query dimension spread.

In general, each element of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pq}$ can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
[\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pq}]_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{i} L_{ik} L_{(3+j),k} = \sum_{k=1}^{i-1} \ell_{ik} \ell_{(3+j),k} + s_i \cdot \ell_{(3+j),i}.
\end{equation}

Cross-terms from spatial correlations $\ell_{ik}$ (where $k < i$) accumulate in the displacement. The displacement in row $i$ satisfies:
\begin{equation}
\|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{pq}^{(i)}\|_2 \leq \sqrt{C}(i-1 + s_i).
\end{equation}
For large $s_i$, this approximates $s_i \sqrt{C}$, but the constant offset $(i-1)\sqrt{C}$ can dominate for small Gaussians. This bound also introduces an asymmetry across spatial dimensions: even when $s_1 = s_2 = s_3$, row $i$ accumulates more cross-terms than row 1, allowing arbitrarily different displacement magnitudes depending on the spatial dimension ordering. While N-DGS uses zero initialization of $\ell_{ij}$ to ensure displacement initially scales with $s_i$, training can learn non-zero correlations that introduce both the offset and this asymmetry.

\textbf{Our method: Explicit coupling via diagonal scaling.} We directly parameterize $\mathbf{V}_{pq} = \mathbf{S} \boldsymbol{\Theta}_{pq}$, where $\mathbf{S} = \text{diag}(s_x, s_y, s_z)$ is the diagonal scale matrix and $\boldsymbol{\Theta}_{pq} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times C}$ are learnable parameters with $\tanh(\cdot)$ activation. This gives:
\begin{equation}
\|\mathbf{V}_{pq}^{(i)}\|_2 = s_i \|\tanh(\boldsymbol{\Theta}_{i,:})\|_2 \leq s_i \sqrt{C}.
\end{equation}
Each element in row $i$ is explicitly $s_i \cdot \tanh(\theta)$ with no cross-terms from other spatial dimensions, ensuring displacement scales exactly with $s_i$ even for tiny Gaussians.

\textbf{Key difference.} Both methods bound displacement by spatial scale, but differ fundamentally:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{N-DGS}: Displacement emerges \textit{implicitly} from Cholesky structure with bound $\sqrt{C}(i-1 + s_i)$. Cross-terms can allow disproportionately large displacements for small Gaussians and introduce asymmetry across spatial dimensions.
\item \textbf{\modelname{}}: Displacement is \textit{explicitly} parameterized with tight bound $s_i\sqrt{C}$, ensuring proper scaling across all Gaussian sizes and symmetric treatment of all spatial dimensions.
\end{itemize}

Empirically, removing spatial scaling (ablation: \textit{d}GS w/o $\mathbf{S}$) degrades PSNR by 2.59 dB on NeRF Synthetic (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}), confirming that displacement scaling is critical. Our explicit parameterization provides tighter bounds, avoids dimension-ordering artifacts, and enables learnable position-opacity disentanglement via $\lambda_{\text{pos}}$.
