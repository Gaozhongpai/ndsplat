\begin{abstract}
Gaussian Splatting has emerged as a state-of-the-art technique for novel view synthesis, with recent extensions like 6DGS and 7DGS enabling the modeling of complex view-dependent effects and dynamic scenes. However, these advanced formulations rely on high-dimensional covariance matrices that couple all conditioning effects together, introducing a critical bottleneck during training: costly and numerically unstable per-Gaussian matrix inversions required in every forward pass. In this paper, we propose \modelname{}, a lightweight parameterization that overcomes these limitations through \textbf{disentangled direct parameterization}. Our key insight is that the full $(3+C) \times (3+C)$ joint covariance matrix is not necessary---instead, we \textbf{disentangle} conditional Gaussian slicing into three independent components: \textbf{position conditioning} (view/time-dependent displacement), \textbf{opacity conditioning} (attention-based modulation), and \textbf{rotation conditioning} (time-dependent orientation for dynamic scenes). Each component is parameterized directly and efficiently: position uses a bounded displacement matrix $\mathbf{V}_{pq}$ with a shared Cholesky precision factor $\mathbf{L}$, opacity uses the same $\mathbf{L}$ for efficient Mahalanobis distance computation without matrix inversion, and rotation uses quaternion SLERP controlled by temporal attention. This disentanglement eliminates the need for full covariance modeling while maintaining expressiveness, delivering substantial practical advantages: training efficiency through elimination of matrix inversions, numerical stability by design, and modular control over each conditioning effect. \modelname{} provides a practical framework for efficiently training both static scenes with complex view-dependence and large-scale dynamic scenes at high frame rates.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
Novel view synthesis has undergone a paradigm shift with the introduction of 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS), which revolutionized the field by achieving photorealistic quality at real-time rendering speeds. By representing scenes as collections of explicit 3D Gaussians, 3DGS established a new standard for static scene reconstruction. However, real-world scenes are inherently dynamic and complex, exhibiting phenomena that extend far beyond the capabilities of static representations: surfaces that shimmer with specular reflections, objects that move and deform over time, and viewing angles that reveal hidden parallax effects.

Recent work has responded to these challenges with sophisticated extensions that elevate Gaussian representations into higher-dimensional spaces. 6D Gaussian Splatting (6DGS) addresses view-dependent effects by modeling each primitive as a Gaussian in the joint space of position and viewing direction. Building upon this, 7D Gaussian Splatting (7DGS) incorporates temporal dynamics by adding a time dimension, creating a unified framework for both view-dependent effects and motion.

While these higher-dimensional formulations demonstrate remarkable expressive power, they introduce a fundamental computational bottleneck that severely impacts their training. The mathematical framework underlying both 6DGS and 7DGS relies on conditioning high-dimensional Gaussian distributions, which necessitates the inversion of covariance sub-matrices. For 6DGS, this translates to a $3\times3$ matrix inversion for every Gaussian, and for 7DGS, a $4\times4$ inversion. This cubic-complexity operation, performed in every forward pass during optimization, makes training exceptionally costly and slow. Beyond computational costs, these matrix inversions introduce critical numerical instabilities. During optimization, covariance matrices may become ill-conditioned or approach singularity, leading to numerical errors that derail training. This fragility undermines the reliability of these methods. While it is possible to pre-process the model after training to cache the results of these inversions and accelerate inference, this does not address the core issues of training inefficiency and model complexity. The crux of the problem lies in the fundamental design philosophy: they model dynamics implicitly through complex covariance structures, deriving transformations through expensive matrix operations.

In this paper, we introduce \textbf{\modelname{}}, a lightweight framework that resolves this tension through \textbf{disentangled direct parameterization}. The `\textbf{d}' in \modelname{} signifies our core principle: rather than learning complex covariance matrices and \textit{deriving} motion through costly inversions, we \textbf{d}isentangle the conditioning into independent components and \textbf{d}irectly parameterize each one---the \textbf{d}isplacement for position, \textbf{d}ecay for opacity, and \textbf{d}elta for rotation.

Our key insight is that the full $(3+C) \times (3+C)$ joint covariance matrix used in N-DGS is unnecessarily complex. In full covariance models, all effects---position shift, opacity modulation, and covariance adjustment---are entangled through a single matrix that must be inverted. We observe that these effects can be \textbf{disentangled} into independent modules, each with its own efficient parameterization:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Position Conditioning:} We directly parameterize a displacement matrix $\mathbf{V}_{pq}$ and a shared Cholesky precision factor $\mathbf{L}$. Position shift is computed as $\mu_p + \mathbf{V}_{pq} \cdot \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^\top \cdot \boldsymbol{\delta}$, requiring only matrix-vector multiplications. The displacement uses bounded parameterization where magnitude scales with Gaussian size, providing implicit regularization.

\item \textbf{Opacity Conditioning:} Opacity modulation reuses the same Cholesky factor $\mathbf{L}$ to compute $\exp(-\lambda \|\mathbf{L}^\top\boldsymbol{\delta}\|^2)$---equivalent to the Mahalanobis distance but without matrix inversion. Diagonal elements use $\exp(\cdot)$ activation to ensure positive definiteness.

\item \textbf{Rotation Conditioning:} For dynamic scenes ($C=4$), rotation is handled by a completely separate module using quaternion SLERP controlled by time-based attention. This decouples rotational dynamics from view-dependent effects, reflecting the physical intuition that object rotation varies with time but not viewing angle.
\end{enumerate}

This disentanglement is the core contribution of \modelname{}. By separating position, opacity, and rotation into independent modules, we eliminate the need for a full joint covariance matrix and its costly inversion. Each module can be optimized and understood independently, while the shared Cholesky factor $\mathbf{L}$ provides efficient computation for both position and opacity conditioning. Our formulation achieves improvements across three axes: training efficiency by eliminating matrix inversion, numerical stability by construction through exponential activation on Cholesky diagonals, and modular design that enables independent control over each conditioning effect.

\section{Preliminary}

Our work builds upon the family of Gaussian Splatting methods. To contextualize our contribution, we first detail the progression from static 3D models to the more complex 6D and 7D formulations, highlighting the trade-offs between expressive power and computational cost at each step.

\subsection{3D Gaussian Splatting}
3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) revolutionized novel view synthesis by representing scenes as a collection of anisotropic 3D Gaussians. Each Gaussian is a primitive defined by a set of learnable attributes that describe its geometry and appearance. Its geometry is determined by a mean vector \(\mu \in \mathbb{R}^3\) specifying its center in 3D space, and a covariance matrix \(\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}\) defining its shape, scale, and orientation. To ensure physical plausibility and simplify optimization, \(\Sigma\) is parameterized by a scaling matrix \(S = \operatorname{diag}(s_x, s_y, s_z)\) and a rotation matrix \(R\) (represented as a quaternion), such that \(\Sigma = R S S^\top R^\top\). The appearance of each Gaussian is governed by a scalar opacity \(\alpha \in [0, 1]\) that controls its transparency, and a view-dependent color modeled using Spherical Harmonics (SH). The color \(c\) for a given viewing direction \(d\) is computed as a sum over the SH basis functions \(Y_{\ell m}\):
\begin{equation}
c(d) = \sum_{\ell=0}^{N} \sum_{m=-\ell}^{\ell} \beta_{\ell m} Y_{\ell m}(d),
\end{equation}
where \(\beta_{\ell m}\) are the learnable SH coefficients. 3DGS achieves state-of-the-art quality and real-time rendering for static scenes. However, its representation is fundamentally static and cannot model geometric changes arising from complex view-dependent effects or temporal dynamics.

\subsection{N-Dimensional Gaussian Splatting}

To address the limitations of 3DGS in capturing view-dependent phenomena and temporal dynamics, recent work has extended Gaussian representations into higher-dimensional spaces. We present a unified N-DGS framework that encompasses both 6D Gaussian Splatting (6DGS) for view-dependent effects and 7D Gaussian Splatting (7DGS) for dynamic scenes.

\paragraph{Joint Distribution.} Each primitive is modeled as an N-dimensional Gaussian over the joint space of position (\(X_p \in \mathbb{R}^3\)) and query variables (\(X_q \in \mathbb{R}^C\)):
\begin{align}
X = \begin{pmatrix} X_p \\ X_q \end{pmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(
\begin{pmatrix}
\mu_p \\ \mu_q
\end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix}
\Sigma_{pp} & \Sigma_{pq} \\
\Sigma_{pq}^\top & \Sigma_{qq}
\end{pmatrix}
\right),
\end{align}
where the query dimension $C$ determines the model variant:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{6DGS ($C=3$):} $X_q$ is the view direction, modeling correlations between position and viewing direction.
\item \textbf{7DGS ($C=4$):} $X_q = (X_d, X_t)^\top$ combines view direction and time, capturing both view-dependent and temporal correlations.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Conditional Gaussian Slicing.} To render an image given query $q$ (view direction for 6DGS, or view direction plus time for 7DGS), the N-D Gaussian is conditioned on $X_q = q$. This yields a 3D Gaussian with conditional mean and covariance:
\begin{align}
\mu_{\text{cond}} &= \mu_p + \Sigma_{pq}\,\Sigma_{qq}^{-1}(q-\mu_q), \\
\Sigma_{\text{cond}} &= \Sigma_{pp} - \Sigma_{pq}\,\Sigma_{qq}^{-1}\,\Sigma_{pq}^\top.
\end{align}
The opacity is modulated based on the Mahalanobis distance to the canonical query value:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_{\text{cond}} = \alpha \cdot \exp\left(-\lambda\,(q-\mu_q)^\top \Sigma_{qq}^{-1}(q-\mu_q)\right),
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is a scaling factor (default $\lambda = 0.35$).

\paragraph{Computational Bottleneck.} While this unified formulation is mathematically elegant and expressive, it introduces a critical bottleneck during training: for every Gaussian in every forward pass, it requires computing the inverse of the $C \times C$ matrix $\Sigma_{qq}$. For 6DGS this is an $O(3^3)$ operation, and for 7DGS an $O(4^3)$ operation per Gaussian. Beyond computational cost, these matrix inversions are susceptible to numerical instability when $\Sigma_{qq}$ becomes ill-conditioned during optimization. Although $\Sigma_{pq}\Sigma_{qq}^{-1}$ and the conditional covariance can be pre-computed after training to accelerate inference, the training process itself remains fundamentally inefficient. This escalating complexity motivates our search for a more efficient and stable parameterization.

\section{Our Approach}

The full covariance formulations of 6DGS and 7DGS model all conditioning effects through a single $(3+C) \times (3+C)$ joint covariance matrix. This entanglement is fundamentally inefficient: to compute any single effect (position shift, opacity modulation, or covariance adjustment), one must invert the entire $C \times C$ conditioning block $\Sigma_{qq}$.

We propose \modelname{}, which \textbf{disentangles} these effects into three independent modules. Rather than deriving all transformations from a shared covariance structure, we directly parameterize each conditioning component separately. This disentanglement eliminates the need for full covariance modeling while enabling efficient, modular computation.

\subsection{Overview}

Our approach reformulates conditional Gaussian slicing into three \textbf{disentangled} components. Given the deviation $\boldsymbol{\delta} = q - \mu_q$ from the canonical view/time:
\begin{align}
\mu_{\text{cond}} &= \mu_p + \mathbf{V}_{pq} \cdot \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^\top \cdot \boldsymbol{\delta} & &\text{(Position)} \\
\alpha_{\text{cond}} &= \alpha \cdot \exp\left(-\lambda \cdot \|\mathbf{L}^\top \boldsymbol{\delta}\|_2^2\right) & &\text{(Opacity)} \\
\mathbf{q}_{\text{cond}} &= \mathbf{q}_{\text{base}} \otimes \text{SLERP}(\mathbf{I}, \mathbf{q}_{\Delta}, 1 - \alpha_{\text{rot}}) & &\text{(Rotation, 7DGS)} \\
\Sigma_{\text{cond}} &= \Sigma_p & &\text{(Covariance)}
\end{align}
where $\mathbf{V}_{pq} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times C}$ is the displacement matrix, $\mathbf{L} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times C}$ is the Cholesky factor of the precision matrix, $\lambda$ is a scaling factor (default 0.35), and $\alpha_{\text{rot}} = \exp(-(t - \mu_t)^2 \cdot \exp(\ell_t^{\text{rot}})^2)$ is the time-based attention weight.

\subsection{Position Conditioning}

In N-DGS, position is computed as $\mu_{\text{cond}} = \mu_p + \Sigma_{pq}\Sigma_{qq}^{-1}(q-\mu_q)$, where $\Sigma_{pq}$ is derived from a joint Cholesky decomposition. Crucially, the displacement is implicitly bounded: since the full covariance matrix is parameterized via Cholesky decomposition $\Sigma = LL^\top$, we have
\begin{equation}
\Sigma_{pq} = L_p L_q^\top,
\end{equation}
where $L_p \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times (3+C)}$ and $L_q \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times (3+C)}$ are submatrices of $L$. This couples $\Sigma_{pq}$ to the spatial covariance $\Sigma_{pp}$, ensuring that small Gaussians (small $\Sigma_{pp}$) produce small displacements.

We directly parameterize the precision matrix $\mathbf{V}_{qq}^{-1} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^\top$ via its Cholesky factor $\mathbf{L} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times C}$, with diagonal elements using exponential activation $L_{ii} = \exp(\ell_{ii})$ to ensure positive definiteness. To preserve the bounded displacement property, we parameterize the displacement matrix $\mathbf{V}_{pq} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times C}$ as:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{V}_{pq} = \text{reshape}\left(\frac{\mathbf{u}}{\|\mathbf{u}\|_2}\right) \cdot \sigma(\gamma) \cdot \lambda_{\max} \cdot \bar{s},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{u} \in \mathbb{R}^{3C}$ is a learnable direction vector (normalized), $\sigma(\gamma) \in [0, 1]$ via sigmoid activation, $\lambda_{\max}$ is the maximum displacement factor (default: 1.0), and $\bar{s} = \frac{1}{3}(s_x + s_y + s_z)$ is the mean spatial scale. This explicit scaling by $\bar{s}$ mimics the implicit coupling in N-DGS: small Gaussians produce small displacements, while large Gaussians have proportionally larger displacement capacity.

\subsection{Opacity Conditioning}

In N-DGS, opacity is modulated as $\alpha_{\text{cond}} = \alpha \cdot \exp(-\lambda\,(q-\mu_q)^\top \Sigma_{qq}^{-1}(q-\mu_q))$, requiring the inverse of $\Sigma_{qq}$, an $O(C^3)$ operation.

Our formulation computes $\mathbf{L}^\top \boldsymbol{\delta}$ followed by $\|\cdot\|_2^2$, requiring only $O(C^2)$ operations (triangular matrix-vector multiply) and is highly parallelizable on GPUs. By parameterizing the Cholesky factor directly with exponential activation on the diagonal, we guarantee that $\mathbf{V}_{qq}^{-1} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^\top$ is always symmetric positive definite, eliminating numerical instabilities from ill-conditioned covariance matrices.

\subsection{Rotation Conditioning}

The original 6DGS does not support rotation conditioning. In 7DGS, rotation is handled implicitly through the Adaptive Gaussian Refinement (AGR) module using MLPs: $\hat{l} = l + \phi_l(f)$, coupling rotation with all covariance parameters.

For dynamic scenes ($C=4$), we introduce an independent rotation module. Unlike position and opacity which depend on both view direction and time, rotation uses \textit{only the time dimension}, reflecting the physical intuition that object rotation varies with time but not viewing angle. Each Gaussian stores a base rotation $\mathbf{q}_{\text{base}}$, a rotation delta $\mathbf{q}_{\Delta}$, and a time-specific precision $\ell_t^{\text{rot}}$. At canonical time $\mu_t$, $\alpha_{\text{rot}} \approx 1$ and $\mathbf{q}_{\text{cond}} = \mathbf{q}_{\text{base}}$; as time deviates, the full delta is progressively applied via SLERP. This provides physical consistency (rotation varies with time, not view), parameter efficiency (only 5 additional parameters), and smooth dynamics on SO(3).

\subsection{Initialization}

Proper initialization is critical:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{View mean} $\mu_d$: Random unit vectors, time in $[0, 1]$ for 7DGS.
\item \textbf{Direction} $\mathbf{u}$: $\mathcal{N}(0, 0.01^2)$, normalized during forward pass.
\item \textbf{Scale} $\gamma$: 0, giving $\sigma(0) = 0.5$ for moderate displacement.
\item \textbf{Cholesky} $\mathbf{L}$: Diagonal $\log(2.0)$, off-diagonal 0.
\item \textbf{Rotation delta} $\mathbf{q}_{\Delta}$: Identity with small noise on imaginary components.
\item \textbf{Rotation precision} $\ell_t^{\text{rot}}$: $\log(\sqrt{2})$ for moderate time sensitivity.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Computational Analysis}

The benefits of our direct Cholesky parameterization are substantial across three critical axes:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Training Efficiency:} We replace $O(C^3)$ matrix inversions with $O(C^2)$ triangular matrix-vector multiplications. While asymptotically similar for small $C$, the practical difference is significant: matrix inversion requires solving a linear system with pivoting and numerical checks, while $\mathbf{L}^\top \boldsymbol{\delta}$ is a simple triangular solve that maps directly to highly optimized BLAS routines and CUDA kernels with no branching or numerical edge cases.

\item \textbf{Numerical Stability:} By parameterizing the Cholesky factor directly with exponential activation on the diagonal, we guarantee that $\mathbf{V}_{qq}^{-1} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^\top$ is always symmetric positive definite. This eliminates the numerical instabilities that arise when covariance matrices become ill-conditioned during optimization---a common failure mode in the original formulation.

\item \textbf{Unified Framework:} Our approach enables a single fused CUDA kernel that computes conditional position, opacity, and rotation in one pass. This minimizes memory bandwidth and intermediate allocations, providing practical speedups beyond the theoretical complexity reduction.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{CUDA Implementation}

We implement the full conditional slicing operation as a custom CUDA kernel (\texttt{slice\_gaussian\_full}) that fuses position, opacity, and rotation computations in a single pass:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Load parameters:} Per-Gaussian $\mu_p$, $\mu_q$, $\mathbf{V}_{pq}$, Cholesky factor $\mathbf{L}$, base rotation $\mathbf{q}_{\text{base}}$, rotation delta $\mathbf{q}_{\Delta}$, rotation precision $\ell_t^{\text{rot}}$
\item \textbf{Compute deviation:} $\boldsymbol{\delta} = \text{query} - \mu_q$
\item \textbf{Build precision matrix:} Reconstruct lower triangular $\mathbf{L}$ from stored parameters, applying $\exp(\cdot)$ to diagonal elements
\item \textbf{Compute $\mathbf{L}^\top\boldsymbol{\delta}$:} Triangular matrix-vector multiply
\item \textbf{Compute opacity scale:} $\alpha_{\text{scale}} = \exp(-\lambda \cdot \|\mathbf{L}^\top\boldsymbol{\delta}\|^2)$
\item \textbf{Compute $\mathbf{L}(\mathbf{L}^\top\boldsymbol{\delta})$:} Second triangular matrix-vector multiply to get $\mathbf{V}_{qq}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\delta}$
\item \textbf{Compute conditional position:} $\mu_{\text{cond}} = \mu_p + \mathbf{V}_{pq} \cdot (\mathbf{V}_{qq}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\delta})$
\item \textbf{Compute rotation (if $C=4$):} Time attention $\alpha_{\text{rot}}$, SLERP interpolation, quaternion composition
\end{enumerate}

The kernel is templated on dimension $C$ (3 or 4) with compile-time loop unrolling for optimal performance. All matrix operations use registers rather than shared memory, as the small matrix sizes ($3\times3$ or $4\times4$) fit entirely in per-thread registers. This fused design minimizes global memory bandwidth by avoiding intermediate tensor allocations and enables efficient parallelization across millions of Gaussians.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented \modelname{}, a disentangled direct parameterization approach for efficient dynamic Gaussian splatting. Our key insight is that the full $(3+C) \times (3+C)$ joint covariance matrix used in N-DGS is unnecessarily complex---the conditioning effects (position shift, opacity modulation, rotation) can be \textbf{disentangled} into independent modules, each with its own efficient parameterization.

By separating these components, we eliminate the need for costly matrix inversions entirely. Position and opacity conditioning share a direct Cholesky precision factor $\mathbf{L}$, while rotation conditioning operates as an independent module using quaternion SLERP. This modular design enables efficient computation through simple matrix-vector multiplications, guarantees numerical stability through exponential activation on diagonal elements, and provides independent control over each conditioning effect.

\modelname{} makes training of large-scale dynamic and view-dependent Gaussian splatting models more tractable, opening new possibilities for real-time applications and interactive experiences.
