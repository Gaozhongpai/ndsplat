\begin{abstract}
Gaussian Splatting has emerged as a state-of-the-art technique for novel view synthesis, with recent extensions like 6DGS and 7DGS enabling the modeling of complex view-dependent effects and dynamic scenes. However, these advanced formulations rely on high-dimensional covariance matrices, which introduce a critical bottleneck during training: costly and numerically unstable per-Gaussian matrix inversions required in every forward pass. In this paper, we propose \modelname{}, a lightweight parameterization that overcomes these limitations through \textbf{direct parameterization}---learning the transformation matrices explicitly rather than deriving them from full covariance structures. Our approach introduces two key innovations. First, a \textbf{diagonal precision parameterization} replaces the full precision matrix with independent per-axis decay factors, reducing precision parameters from $\mathcal{O}(C^2)$ to $\mathcal{O}(C)$ while enabling element-wise operations instead of matrix inversions. Second, a \textbf{bounded displacement parameterization} decomposes the position-view covariance into a normalized direction and a sigmoid-bounded magnitude scaled by each Gaussian's spatial extent, providing implicit structural regularization where small Gaussians produce small displacements. Our approach achieves equivalent expressive power for linear dynamics while delivering substantial practical advantages: training efficiency through elimination of cubic-time matrix inversions, numerical stability by design, and improved parameter efficiency with a more compact model. \modelname{} provides a practical framework for efficiently training both static scenes with complex view-dependence and large-scale dynamic scenes at high frame rates.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
Novel view synthesis has undergone a paradigm shift with the introduction of 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS), which revolutionized the field by achieving photorealistic quality at real-time rendering speeds. By representing scenes as collections of explicit 3D Gaussians, 3DGS established a new standard for static scene reconstruction. However, real-world scenes are inherently dynamic and complex, exhibiting phenomena that extend far beyond the capabilities of static representations: surfaces that shimmer with specular reflections, objects that move and deform over time, and viewing angles that reveal hidden parallax effects.

Recent work has responded to these challenges with sophisticated extensions that elevate Gaussian representations into higher-dimensional spaces. 6D Gaussian Splatting (6DGS) addresses view-dependent effects by modeling each primitive as a Gaussian in the joint space of position and viewing direction. Building upon this, 7D Gaussian Splatting (7DGS) incorporates temporal dynamics by adding a time dimension, creating a unified framework for both view-dependent effects and motion.

While these higher-dimensional formulations demonstrate remarkable expressive power, they introduce a fundamental computational bottleneck that severely impacts their training. The mathematical framework underlying both 6DGS and 7DGS relies on conditioning high-dimensional Gaussian distributions, which necessitates the inversion of covariance sub-matrices. For 6DGS, this translates to a $3\times3$ matrix inversion for every Gaussian, and for 7DGS, a $4\times4$ inversion. This cubic-complexity operation, performed in every forward pass during optimization, makes training exceptionally costly and slow. Beyond computational costs, these matrix inversions introduce critical numerical instabilities. During optimization, covariance matrices may become ill-conditioned or approach singularity, leading to numerical errors that derail training. This fragility undermines the reliability of these methods. While it is possible to pre-process the model after training to cache the results of these inversions and accelerate inference, this does not address the core issues of training inefficiency and model complexity. The crux of the problem lies in the fundamental design philosophy: they model dynamics implicitly through complex covariance structures, deriving transformations through expensive matrix operations.

In this paper, we introduce \textbf{\modelname{}}, a lightweight framework that resolves this tension through \textbf{direct parameterization}. The `\textbf{d}' in \modelname{} signifies our core principle: rather than learning complex covariance matrices and \textit{deriving} motion through costly inversions, we \textbf{d}irectly parameterize the essential quantities needed for conditional Gaussian slicing---the \textbf{d}isplacement matrices and \textbf{d}ecay factors. Our approach centers on two key innovations:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Diagonal Precision Parameterization:} We replace the full $C \times C$ precision matrix (where $C=3$ for view-only or $C=4$ for view+time) with $C$ independent diagonal elements. Each element $\exp(d_i)^2$ controls the decay rate along its corresponding axis, enabling the entire opacity modulation and displacement computation to use element-wise operations rather than matrix inversions.

\item \textbf{Bounded Displacement Parameterization:} We decompose the position-view covariance block $\mathbf{V}_{12}$ into a normalized direction vector and a sigmoid-bounded magnitude that scales with each Gaussian's spatial extent. This provides implicit structural regularization: small Gaussians can only produce small position shifts, while large Gaussians have proportionally larger shift capacity---mimicking the implicit constraint in full covariance models without the computational overhead.
\end{enumerate}

Critically, \modelname{} is not an approximation---it represents a functionally equivalent reparameterization of linear dynamics that bypasses the need for full N-DGS covariance modeling. Our formulation achieves improvements across three axes: training efficiency by eliminating cubic-time operations in favor of linear-time element-wise products, numerical stability by avoiding matrix inversion entirely, and parameter efficiency via a more compact model structure.

\section{Preliminary}

Our work builds upon the family of Gaussian Splatting methods. To contextualize our contribution, we first detail the progression from static 3D models to the more complex 6D and 7D formulations, highlighting the trade-offs between expressive power and computational cost at each step.

\subsection{3D Gaussian Splatting}
3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) revolutionized novel view synthesis by representing scenes as a collection of anisotropic 3D Gaussians. Each Gaussian is a primitive defined by a set of learnable attributes that describe its geometry and appearance. Its geometry is determined by a mean vector \(\mu \in \mathbb{R}^3\) specifying its center in 3D space, and a covariance matrix \(\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}\) defining its shape, scale, and orientation. To ensure physical plausibility and simplify optimization, \(\Sigma\) is parameterized by a scaling matrix \(S = \operatorname{diag}(s_x, s_y, s_z)\) and a rotation matrix \(R\) (represented as a quaternion), such that \(\Sigma = R S S^\top R^\top\). The appearance of each Gaussian is governed by a scalar opacity \(\alpha \in [0, 1]\) that controls its transparency, and a view-dependent color modeled using Spherical Harmonics (SH). The color \(c\) for a given viewing direction \(d\) is computed as a sum over the SH basis functions \(Y_{\ell m}\):
\begin{equation}
c(d) = \sum_{\ell=0}^{N} \sum_{m=-\ell}^{\ell} \beta_{\ell m} Y_{\ell m}(d),
\end{equation}
where \(\beta_{\ell m}\) are the learnable SH coefficients. 3DGS achieves state-of-the-art quality and real-time rendering for static scenes. However, its representation is fundamentally static and cannot model geometric changes arising from complex view-dependent effects or temporal dynamics.

\subsection{6D Gaussian Splatting: Modeling View-Dependent Effects}
To address the limitations of 3DGS in capturing view-dependent phenomena such as parallax and specular reflections, 6D Gaussian Splatting (6DGS) extends the representation into a 6D space. Each primitive is modeled as a 6D Gaussian over the joint space of position (\(X_p \in \mathbb{R}^3\)) and view direction (\(X_d \in \mathbb{R}^3\)). The joint distribution is:
\begin{align}
X = \begin{pmatrix} X_p \\ X_d \end{pmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(
\begin{pmatrix}
\mu_p \\ \mu_d
\end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix}
\Sigma_p & \Sigma_{pd} \\
\Sigma_{pd}^\top & \Sigma_d
\end{pmatrix}
\right).
\end{align}
Here, the cross-covariance matrix \(\Sigma_{pd}\) models the correlation between a point's spatial properties and its viewing direction. To render an image from a direction \(d\), the 6D Gaussian is conditioned on \(X_d = d\). This yields a 3D Gaussian whose properties are dynamically adjusted. The conditional mean and covariance are given by:
\begin{align}
\mu_{\text{cond}}^{6D} &= \mu_p + \Sigma_{pd}\,\Sigma_d^{-1}(d-\mu_d), \\
\Sigma_{\text{cond}}^{6D} &= \Sigma_p - \Sigma_{pd}\,\Sigma_d^{-1}\,\Sigma_{pd}^\top.
\end{align}
Additionally, the opacity is modulated based on the Mahalanobis distance:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_{\text{cond}} = \alpha \cdot \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}\,(d-\mu_d)^\top \Sigma_d^{-1}(d-\mu_d)\right).
\end{equation}
While expressive, this formulation introduces a significant bottleneck during training: for every Gaussian, it must compute the inverse of the \(3 \times 3\) matrix \(\Sigma_d\) in each forward pass. This $O(3^3)$ operation is costly and susceptible to numerical instability. Although the matrix \(\Sigma_{pd}\Sigma_d^{-1}\) and the conditional covariance can be pre-computed after training to accelerate inference, the training process itself remains fundamentally inefficient.

\subsection{7D Gaussian Splatting: Incorporating Temporal Dynamics}
To represent dynamic scenes, 7D Gaussian Splatting (7DGS) further extends the joint space to include a temporal dimension (\(X_t \in \mathbb{R}\)). Each scene element is now a 7D Gaussian defined by:
\begin{align}
X = \begin{pmatrix} X_p \\ X_t \\ X_d \end{pmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(
\begin{pmatrix}
\mu_p \\ \mu_t \\ \mu_d
\end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix}
\Sigma_p & \Sigma_{pt} & \Sigma_{pd} \\
\Sigma_{pt}^\top & \Sigma_t & \Sigma_{td} \\
\Sigma_{pd}^\top & \Sigma_{td}^\top & \Sigma_d
\end{pmatrix}
\right).
\end{align}
To render a frame at time \(t\) from direction \(d\), we partition the covariance matrix as:
\[
\Sigma_{(t,d)} = \begin{pmatrix} \Sigma_t & \Sigma_{td} \\ \Sigma_{td}^\top & \Sigma_d \end{pmatrix} \quad \text{and} \quad
\Sigma_{p,(t,d)} = \begin{bmatrix} \Sigma_{pt} & \Sigma_{pd} \end{bmatrix}.
\]
The conditional 3D Gaussian for rendering is then defined by:
\begin{align}
\mu_{\text{cond}}^{7D} &= \mu_p + \Sigma_{p,(t,d)} \, \Sigma_{(t,d)}^{-1} \, \begin{pmatrix} t-\mu_t \\ d-\mu_d \end{pmatrix}, \\
\Sigma_{\text{cond}}^{7D} &= \Sigma_p - \Sigma_{p,(t,d)} \, \Sigma_{(t,d)}^{-1} \, \Sigma_{p,(t,d)}^\top.
\end{align}
Opacity is modulated similarly to 6DGS. This formulation exacerbates the issues of 6DGS. The training process now requires inverting the \(4 \times 4\) matrix \(\Sigma_{(t,d)}\), an $O(4^3)$ operation per-Gaussian, making training even more computationally intensive and prone to instability. This escalating complexity motivates our search for a more efficient and stable parameterization.

\section{Our Approach}

The full covariance formulations of 6DGS and 7DGS, while expressive, are fundamentally constrained by a design that is inefficient for training: they model dynamic effects \textit{implicitly} through high-dimensional covariance matrices and derive transformations via costly matrix inversions. We propose \modelname{}, a lightweight framework that replaces this implicit modeling with explicit, efficient parameterizations that maintain expressive power while dramatically improving training efficiency and stability.

\subsection{Core Insight: Direct vs. Derived Parameterization}

The central insight of our method is that the conditional mean computation in 6DGS/7DGS can be decomposed into two independent components:
\begin{equation}
\mu_{\text{cond}} = \mu_p + \underbrace{\mathbf{V}_{12}}_{\text{displacement direction}} \cdot \underbrace{\mathbf{V}_{22}^{-1} \cdot \boldsymbol{\delta}}_{\text{scaled deviation}},
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\delta} = (d - \mu_d)$ for 6DGS or $\boldsymbol{\delta} = (t - \mu_t, d - \mu_d)^\top$ for 7DGS. In full N-DGS formulations, $\mathbf{V}_{12}$ is \textit{derived} from covariance terms (via $\mathbf{V}_{12} = \mathbf{L}_{11}\mathbf{L}_{21}^\top$) and $\mathbf{V}_{22}^{-1}$ requires explicit matrix inversion. Our key observation is that these quantities can instead be parameterized \textit{directly}:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The displacement matrix $\mathbf{V}_{12}$ can be learned directly with appropriate structural constraints, bypassing the need for full Cholesky decomposition.
\item The precision matrix $\mathbf{V}_{22}^{-1}$ can be restricted to a diagonal form, enabling element-wise operations instead of matrix inversion.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Diagonal Precision Parameterization}

Instead of learning a full $C \times C$ covariance matrix $\Sigma_{22}$ (where $C=3$ for view or $C=4$ for view+time) and computing its inverse, we directly parameterize the precision matrix as a diagonal:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{V}_{22}^{-1} = \text{diag}\left(\exp(d_0)^2, \exp(d_1)^2, \ldots, \exp(d_{C-1})^2\right),
\end{equation}
where $\{d_i\}_{i=0}^{C-1}$ are learnable scalar parameters per Gaussian. The exponential-squared activation ensures positive definiteness while providing a natural parameterization of precision (inverse variance) along each axis.

\paragraph{Opacity Modulation.} The Mahalanobis-based opacity modulation simplifies to a weighted sum:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_{\text{cond}} = \alpha \cdot \exp\left(- \sum_{i=0}^{C-1} (\delta_i \cdot \exp(d_i))^2\right),
\end{equation}
where $\delta_i$ is the $i$-th component of the deviation vector. Note that the $\frac{1}{2}$ factor from the standard Gaussian is absorbed into the learnable $d_i$ parameters. This replaces the quadratic form $\boldsymbol{\delta}^\top \Sigma_{22}^{-1} \boldsymbol{\delta}$ with $C$ independent element-wise multiplications and a sum---a dramatic reduction in computational complexity.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} Each $\exp(d_i)^2$ acts as an independent decay rate controlling how quickly the Gaussian's opacity falls off as the query deviates from the mean along axis $i$. For view-only models ($C=3$), the three parameters control sensitivity to changes in the $x$, $y$, and $z$ components of the viewing direction. For dynamic models ($C=4$), an additional parameter controls temporal sensitivity.

\subsection{Bounded Displacement Parameterization}

For the position-view covariance block $\mathbf{V}_{12} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times C}$, we introduce a decomposition that provides implicit structural regularization:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{V}_{12} = \text{reshape}\left(\frac{\mathbf{u}}{\|\mathbf{u}\|_2}\right) \cdot \sigma(\gamma) \cdot \lambda_{\max} \cdot \bar{s},
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\mathbf{u} \in \mathbb{R}^{3C}$ is a learnable direction vector, normalized to unit norm
\item $\gamma \in \mathbb{R}$ is a learnable scale parameter, with $\sigma(\gamma) \in [0, 1]$ via sigmoid activation
\item $\lambda_{\max}$ is a hyperparameter controlling the maximum displacement factor (default: 1.0)
\item $\bar{s} = \frac{1}{3}(s_x + s_y + s_z)$ is the mean spatial scale of the Gaussian
\end{itemize}

The reshaped normalized direction vector is multiplied by the bounded magnitude $\sigma(\gamma) \cdot \lambda_{\max} \cdot \bar{s}$ to produce the final $3 \times C$ displacement matrix.

\paragraph{Structural Regularization.} This parameterization enforces an important structural constraint: the maximum position displacement is proportional to the Gaussian's spatial extent. Small, detailed Gaussians can only shift by small amounts, while large Gaussians covering broader regions can shift more. This mimics the implicit constraint in full covariance models where $\mathbf{V}_{12} = \mathbf{L}_{11} \mathbf{L}_{21}^\top$ couples the displacement magnitude to the spatial covariance structure.

\paragraph{Bounded Gradients.} By using sigmoid activation for the magnitude, we ensure that the displacement magnitude remains bounded during optimization, preventing the explosive gradients that can occur with unbounded parameterizations.

\subsection{Complete Conditional Slicing}

Combining the diagonal precision and bounded displacement parameterizations, the complete conditional position computation becomes:
\begin{equation}
\mu_{\text{cond}} = \mu_p + \mathbf{V}_{12} \cdot \left(\mathbf{V}_{22}^{-1} \odot \boldsymbol{\delta}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\odot$ denotes element-wise multiplication. Expanding this for the 7DGS case (view + time):
\begin{equation}
\mu_{\text{cond}} = \mu_p + \mathbf{v}_t \cdot \exp(d_t)^2 \cdot (t - \mu_t) + \mathbf{V}_d \cdot \left(\exp(\mathbf{d}_v)^2 \odot (d - \mu_d)\right),
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{v}_t \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is the temporal velocity component and $\mathbf{V}_d \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$ is the view-dependent displacement matrix, both derived from the bounded $\mathbf{V}_{12}$.

For the 6DGS case (view only), the temporal term is omitted:
\begin{equation}
\mu_{\text{cond}} = \mu_p + \mathbf{V}_d \cdot \left(\exp(\mathbf{d}_v)^2 \odot (d - \mu_d)\right).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Static Conditional Covariance.} Following the insight that position shifts are the primary driver of view-dependent and temporal effects, we keep the conditional covariance static:
\begin{equation}
\Sigma_{\text{cond}} = \Sigma_p.
\end{equation}
This simplification focuses the model's dynamic capacity on the conditional mean, which is most critical for representing motion and parallax, while avoiding the additional complexity of conditional covariance computation.

\subsection{Initialization and Regularization}

Proper initialization is critical for stable optimization of our lightweight parameters.

\paragraph{Initialization Strategy.}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{View mean} $\mu_d$: Random unit vectors on the sphere, with optional time component uniformly sampled in $[0, 1]$ for 7DGS.
\item \textbf{Direction} $\mathbf{u}$: Small random values $\mathcal{N}(0, 0.01^2)$, normalized during forward pass.
\item \textbf{Scale} $\gamma$: Initialized to 0, so $\sigma(0) = 0.5$ gives moderate initial displacement capacity.
\item \textbf{Diagonal precision} $\{d_i\}$: Initialized to $\log(\sqrt{2}) \approx 0.347$, giving $\exp(d_i)^2 \approx 2.0$ for moderate opacity decay.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Regularization.} We apply light regularization to encourage smooth solutions:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{reg}} = \lambda_{\text{diag}} \sum_{i=0}^{C-1} \exp(d_i)^4 + \lambda_{\sigma} \cdot \frac{1}{\sigma(\gamma) + \epsilon},
\end{equation}
where the first term penalizes extremely sharp precision (very fast decay), and the second term prevents the displacement magnitude from collapsing to zero. We use $\lambda_{\text{diag}} = 0.001$ and $\lambda_{\sigma} = 0.0001$ in practice.

The benefits are substantial across three critical axes:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Training Efficiency:} We replace $O(C^3)$ matrix inversions with $O(C)$ element-wise operations. For $C=4$ (7DGS), this represents a theoretical 16$\times$ reduction in per-Gaussian computation for the conditioning step, with even larger practical speedups due to better GPU utilization of element-wise operations.

\item \textbf{Numerical Stability:} By parameterizing precision directly as $\exp(d_i)^2$, we guarantee positive values without the risk of ill-conditioned or singular matrices. The exponential parameterization also provides natural gradient scaling---small changes in $d_i$ produce proportional changes in precision.

\item \textbf{Parameter Efficiency:} Our diagonal parameterization uses fewer parameters (13 vs 15 for 6DGS, 17 vs 22 for 7DGS) while providing explicit control over each axis's behavior. This reduces memory footprint and can accelerate training convergence.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{CUDA Implementation}

We implement the diagonal slicing operation as a custom CUDA kernel (\texttt{slice\_gaussian\_simple\_diag}) that fuses the conditional position and opacity computations:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Load per-Gaussian parameters: $\mu_p$, $\mu_v$, $\mathbf{V}_{12}$, $\{d_i\}$
\item Compute deviation: $\boldsymbol{\delta} = \text{query} - \mu_v$
\item Compute diagonal precision: $L_{\text{diag},i} = \exp(d_i)$, $\text{prec}_i = L_{\text{diag},i}^2$
\item Compute scaled deviation: $\boldsymbol{\delta}_{\text{scaled}} = \text{prec} \odot \boldsymbol{\delta}$
\item Compute conditional position: $\mu_{\text{cond}} = \mu_p + \mathbf{V}_{12} \cdot \boldsymbol{\delta}_{\text{scaled}}$
\item Compute opacity scale: $\text{scale} = \exp\left(-\sum_i (\delta_i \cdot L_{\text{diag},i})^2\right)$
\end{enumerate}

This fused kernel minimizes memory bandwidth by avoiding intermediate tensor allocations and enables efficient parallelization across Gaussians.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented \modelname{}, a direct parameterization approach for efficient dynamic Gaussian splatting that addresses the computational and numerical challenges of full N-DGS covariance formulations. By directly learning displacement matrices and diagonal precision factors instead of deriving them from full covariance structures, \modelname{} achieves substantial improvements in training efficiency and stability while maintaining expressive power for linear view-dependent and temporal dynamics. Our approach makes training of large-scale dynamic and view-dependent Gaussian splatting models more tractable, opening new possibilities for real-time applications and interactive experiences.
